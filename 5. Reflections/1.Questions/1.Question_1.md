## Q1 — The Packet That Changed My Interpretation

> *Identify a specific packet, frame, or flow in your chosen capture that changed how you thought about what was happening. Cite the packet number or time offset, the protocol-level detail that mattered, and what your interpretation was before versus after.*

### The Packet

**Frame 10799 · Time offset: T+484.409 seconds · Protocol: DCE/RPC → `svcctl`**

```
frame.number  frame.time_relative  ip.src         ip.dst
10799         484.409249           10.5.26.132    10.5.26.4
```

**PCAP:** `2021-05-26-Trickbot-infection-with-Cobalt-Strike.pcap`

---

### Before — What I Thought Was Happening

- My read up to that point was a **single-host infection story**
- The workstation `10.5.26.132` was clearly compromised — it had already fired three data exfiltration POSTs to `36.95.27.243` and `103.102.220.50` by frame 3045 (T+391s), embedding its own hostname in the URI path (`/rob87/DESKTOP-OG16DGY_W617601.../90`)
- The domain controller `10.5.26.4` had SMB traffic, but I attributed this to **normal DC-to-workstation file sharing** — DCs always have SMB activity, so I was not treating it as suspicious
- My working hypothesis was: one infected workstation, beaconing out, exfiltrating credentials — contained to that single machine

---

### The Protocol Detail That Mattered

Frame 10799 is a **DCE/RPC `svcctl` call** — specifically, the Windows **Service Control Manager** interface over named pipe `\svcctl` via SMB.

`svcctl` is the protocol layer used to **create, start, and stop Windows services on a remote machine**. It is the same mechanism that tools like PsExec use. A workstation initiating `svcctl` calls *to a domain controller* is not file sharing — it is **remote code execution**.

```
Frame 10799:  10.5.26.132  →  10.5.26.4   [svcctl: CreateService]
Frame 10802:  10.5.26.4    →  10.5.26.132 [svcctl: response]
Frame 10803:  10.5.26.132  →  10.5.26.4   [svcctl: StartService]
```

The exchange was a rapid 5-microsecond burst — not a human doing anything interactively, but an automated spreading module firing a pre-written routine.

---

### After — What I Now Understood

- The intrusion was **not contained to the workstation** — it was actively spreading
- The SMB volume confirmed it: the workstation had sent **2,164 SMB packets** to the DC, versus ~120 from every other host — that imbalance had been in the data all along but I had not weighted it correctly until I saw the `svcctl` call
- I checked the DC's first exfiltration POST and found **frame 13210 at T+783s** — the DC began sending its own harvested data (`/tot108/VICTORYPUNK-DC_W617601.../90`) exactly **299 seconds after frame 10799**, which is precisely the time needed for a service to install, execute, and reach its own reporting stage
- The entire SMB traffic I had dismissed as "normal DC activity" was the **lateral movement itself** — the packet I had been looking at but not reading
